# Legal and Compliance Guide for a U.S. Website Services Business

This is operational guidance, not legal advice. It is designed to help you launch more carefully and know what should be reviewed with a local attorney or CPA.

## Working Assumption

This package assumes:

- you are in the United States,
- you are starting as a solo founder,
- you are selling brochure-style website services,
- you will reach out to businesses directly,
- you may build draft websites based on publicly available business facts.

## 1. Business Formation and Registration

At a minimum, you need to decide how you will operate:

- sole proprietorship,
- single-member LLC,
- corporation with a tax election if your advisor recommends it.

For many solo service businesses, a single-member LLC is common because it can help separate business and personal liability, but the right structure depends on your state, tax situation, and risk tolerance.

Operational rule:

- form the entity first if you are creating an LLC or corporation,
- then get the EIN,
- then open the business bank account,
- then sign client contracts in the business name.

## 2. BOI Reporting Status

This changed recently.

As of March 26, 2025, FinCEN says entities created in the United States and their beneficial owners are exempt from the federal BOI reporting requirement under the Corporate Transparency Act. That means a typical U.S.-formed solo LLC is currently not required to file BOI under the revised federal rule.

Important:

- this is a current-status item, not a forever item,
- re-check it before formation in case the rule changes again.

## 3. Taxes and Money Handling

If you are self-employed, the IRS says you generally must file an annual return and pay estimated taxes quarterly when required.

Operational rule:

- keep business and personal funds separate,
- keep receipts,
- track software, hosting, contractor, and mileage expenses,
- set aside tax money from each payment,
- talk to a CPA about entity choice and quarterly payments.

## 4. Can You Build a Website from Public Information?

Short answer: you can use public facts as research input, but you should not assume that all publicly visible content is free to copy.

Safer to use as factual input:

- business name,
- address,
- phone number,
- hours,
- service area,
- service categories,
- booking links,
- general factual descriptions of services.

Higher-risk content that you should not copy without permission or license:

- logos,
- photos,
- videos,
- social media graphics,
- written marketing copy,
- blog posts,
- menus with creative formatting and design elements,
- customer reviews used as testimonials,
- branded slogans.

Why:

- the U.S. Copyright Office says copyright protects original works of authorship,
- it does not protect facts, but it does protect original expression,
- changing someone else's work does not automatically make it yours.

Operational rule:

- use public information to understand the business,
- write your own original layout and draft copy,
- use placeholders or licensed stock until the owner approves the final content,
- never publish copied assets just because they were easy to find online.

## 5. Best Legal Version of Your Business Model

The lower-risk version of your model is:

1. Research a business with no website.
2. Build a private draft, mockup, or concept page using original design and copy structure.
3. Reach out and explain that the draft was informed by public business information.
4. If the owner is interested, move them into a paid project.
5. Replace or confirm all assets before launch.
6. Get written approval before publishing.

Do not:

- publish a live site for a business without consent,
- use their logo or photos without permission,
- imply you are their authorized agent before they hire you,
- claim results or testimonials you cannot support.

## 6. Outreach Rules: Email, Calls, and Texts

### Email

Commercial email is subject to the CAN-SPAM Act. In plain terms, your outreach should:

- identify who you are,
- avoid deceptive subject lines,
- give a real way to opt out,
- honor opt-out requests quickly.

Operational rule:

- use honest subject lines,
- include your real business identity,
- add a simple opt-out line in every outreach email,
- keep a suppression list so you do not re-email people who opted out.

### Calls

The FTC's Telemarketing Sales Rule and the FCC's TCPA rules matter here.

Important nuance:

- business-to-business calls are treated differently from consumer telemarketing in some FTC contexts,
- but the FTC still prohibits misrepresentations and certain abusive practices,
- FCC rules still matter for prerecorded calls, autodialed calls, and calls to wireless numbers.

Operational rule:

- keep outreach to live, manual calls,
- call publicly listed business numbers, not scraped personal cell numbers,
- do not use robocalls or ringless voicemail,
- do not use auto-dialed marketing texts without clear consent,
- stop calling if asked not to call again,
- keep your own do-not-contact list.

This is one of the areas where state law can add extra requirements. If phone outreach becomes a major channel, have counsel review your process.

## 7. Testimonials, Reviews, and Portfolio Claims

The FTC expects endorsements and testimonials to be honest and not misleading.

Operational rule:

- only use real client feedback,
- do not invent testimonials,
- do not buy fake reviews,
- if you give an incentive for a review, understand the disclosure implications,
- if you show a sample project or concept site, label it clearly as a concept if it was not a paid engagement.

## 8. Accessibility

The ADA applies to businesses open to the public, and the DOJ says businesses should make web content accessible to people with disabilities.

For private businesses, the DOJ guidance does not give one mandatory technical standard in the same way the 2024 Title II rule does for state and local governments, but WCAG 2.1 AA is widely used as the practical benchmark.

Operational rule:

- use semantic HTML,
- maintain color contrast,
- add alt text,
- label forms,
- support keyboard use,
- avoid motion that cannot be reduced,
- caption videos when used.

You do not need to promise perfect compliance in your marketing. You do need to avoid selling inaccessible work as if accessibility does not matter.

## 9. Privacy and Security

If you or your clients collect leads through contact forms, appointment forms, or newsletter signup forms, you are handling personal information.

Operational rule:

- collect only the data you actually need,
- use HTTPS,
- use reputable form and hosting vendors,
- keep software updated,
- enable MFA on hosting, domain, and email,
- do not store client credentials in plain text,
- make privacy statements truthful,
- understand that state privacy rules can apply depending on what data is collected and where users are located.

## 10. Client Ownership and Account Control

One of the most important business-protection rules is to make final client assets live in client-owned accounts whenever practical.

Preferred:

- domain registered to the client,
- hosting owned by the client or clearly documented,
- business email owned by the client,
- analytics and search console accessible by the client,
- social accounts always owned by the client.

Why:

- it reduces disputes,
- it makes your service look more professional,
- it prevents hostage-style handoff conflicts,
- it lowers your long-term liability if the relationship ends.

## 11. Contracts You Should Have Before Client Work

Before taking money, have:

- a service agreement,
- a proposal or statement of work,
- a content and asset authorization form,
- an invoice,
- a clear maintenance add-on if you offer ongoing support.

Your agreement should cover:

- scope,
- timeline,
- revisions,
- payment terms,
- client responsibilities,
- content approval,
- asset rights,
- hosting and third-party services,
- limitation of liability,
- termination,
- ownership transfer after final payment.

## 12. Practical Red / Yellow / Green Rules

### Green

- using public facts as research,
- writing original copy based on those facts,
- using licensed stock images,
- creating a private concept site before a client says yes,
- asking the owner to approve and provide final assets.

### Yellow

- paraphrasing public "about us" language,
- referencing public reviews in discovery,
- using screenshots in private sales conversations,
- reaching out through business social accounts.

Use caution and convert yellow items into approved client assets before launch.

### Red

- copying photos from Facebook or Instagram onto a live site,
- using a logo without permission,
- copying page text from social posts or competitors,
- fabricating testimonials,
- publishing without owner approval,
- using robocalls or auto-marketing texts without proper consent,
- claiming legal or accessibility compliance you have not actually delivered.

## 13. What to Ask a Local Attorney or CPA

- Should I start as a sole proprietor or LLC in my state?
- Do I need a local business license where I live or where I sell?
- What contract changes should I make for my state law?
- How should I structure intellectual property transfer and limitation of liability?
- What outreach methods create the most telemarketing risk in my state?
- Should I elect S-corp tax treatment later?
- What insurance coverage should I carry at my size and service mix?
