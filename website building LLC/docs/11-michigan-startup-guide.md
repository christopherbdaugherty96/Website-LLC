# Michigan Startup Guide for Your Website Business

This guide is tailored to you as a Michigan founder who has not formed an LLC yet.

Current date used for this guide: March 13, 2026.

This is practical guidance based on current official sources, not legal or tax advice.

## The Short Answer

You do not need an LLC before you can start.

In Michigan, your two practical starting options are:

1. Start now as a sole proprietor.
2. Form a Michigan LLC before taking clients.

## Which Option Fits Best?

### Option A: Start as a Sole Proprietor

This is the simplest path if you want to start fast and keep costs low.

What it means:

- the business and you are legally the same person,
- business income is generally reported on your personal tax return,
- there is no liability shield between business obligations and your personal assets.

Good fit if:

- you want to test demand first,
- you have not signed clients yet,
- you want the lowest upfront cost.

Main downside:

- there is less legal separation between you and the business than with an LLC.

### Option B: Form a Michigan LLC

This is the cleaner long-term structure if you plan to sign clients, handle hosting, or grow into a real agency.

What it changes:

- the business becomes its own legal entity,
- you file formation paperwork with Michigan,
- you maintain annual filings,
- you get better legal separation if you operate properly.

Good fit if:

- you plan to sign paying clients soon,
- you want cleaner contracts and banking,
- you want a more professional structure from the start.

## If You Stay a Sole Proprietor for Now

### If You Use Your Personal Legal Name

You may be able to operate under your own legal name without forming an LLC.

Example:

- "Chris Smith" offering website design services.

### If You Use a Business Name

If you use a name other than your real name, Michigan says sole proprietorships using an assumed name register with the county clerk.

Practical example:

- if your legal name is Chris Smith but you market as "Main Street Site Co.," treat that as an assumed name issue.

Practical next step:

- call or visit your county clerk and ask about assumed name filing for a sole proprietorship.

Important:

- Michigan does not have one central statewide trade-name registration system for sole proprietors,
- county clerk handling matters here.

## If You Form a Michigan LLC

### Core Filing

Michigan says a domestic LLC is formed by filing Articles of Organization, form `CSCL/CD-700`, with LARA.

As of the current official filing schedule, the filing fee for domestic LLC Articles of Organization is `$50`.

### Required Basics for the LLC Filing

Michigan's LLC guidance says the Articles of Organization must include:

- the LLC name,
- a resident agent,
- a registered office,
- the business purpose or all-purpose clause as allowed.

### Assumed Name for an LLC

If your LLC uses a brand name other than its true legal entity name, Michigan requires a Certificate of Assumed Name for LLCs.

As of the current filing schedule, the Certificate of Assumed Name filing fee for LLCs is `$25`.

Michigan's guide also says the assumed name expires on December 31 of the fifth full calendar year after filing.

### Annual Statement

Michigan requires LLCs to file an Annual Statement.

Current Michigan rule:

- annual statement due date: February 15 each year,
- filing fee: `$25`,
- first annual statement is due the year after formation,
- if the LLC is formed after September 30, no annual statement is due on the immediately following February 15.

### Portal Change You Should Know

Michigan moved business filings to the MiBusiness Registry Portal in 2025.

As of June 23, 2025:

- annual reports and annual statements must be filed online,
- Michigan uses the MiBusiness Registry Portal and MiLogin for Business for entity filings and annuals.

## My Recommendation for You

Because you are offering client services, contracts, websites, domains, and possibly hosting coordination, the cleaner business setup is usually:

1. choose your business name,
2. form a Michigan single-member LLC,
3. get an EIN,
4. open a business bank account,
5. sign all new work through the LLC.

If money is tight and you want to validate demand first, a reasonable practical approach is:

1. start as a sole proprietor,
2. use your personal legal name or properly file an assumed name with your county clerk,
3. get your first one or two paid clients,
4. then form the LLC and move future contracts and banking into it.

That recommendation is a practical judgment call, not a legal conclusion.

## Michigan Step-by-Step Setup Checklist

## Phase 1: Name and Structure

1. Pick the business name you want to use.
2. Search Michigan business records before filing.
3. Decide whether you are launching as a sole proprietor or single-member LLC.
4. If staying a sole proprietor and using a brand name, check assumed name filing with your county clerk.

## Phase 2: Formation

If forming an LLC:

1. Create a MiLogin for Business account.
2. File Articles of Organization through Michigan's business filing portal.
3. List your resident agent and registered office.
4. Save stamped formation documents.

## Phase 3: Federal and Banking

1. Get an EIN from the IRS.
2. Open a business bank account.
3. Keep business income and expenses separate from personal spending.

Even if you start as a sole proprietor, an EIN is still helpful for banking, invoices, and client-facing professionalism.

## Phase 4: Michigan Tax and Employer Accounts

Michigan Treasury says business tax registration is done through Michigan Treasury Online or Form 518 by mail, and an FEIN is required for online registration.

Practical rule:

- if Treasury registration applies to your business situation, complete it through MTO,
- if you are unsure which taxes apply, ask a CPA before guessing.

I am intentionally not telling you that all web-design-only businesses must register for every Michigan business tax account, because that depends on your exact activity, whether you sell taxable items, and whether you hire workers.

## Phase 5: Licensing

Use Michigan's State License Search and your local city, township, or county offices to confirm whether you need:

- a local business license,
- a home occupation permit,
- zoning approval,
- any niche-specific permit tied to your location or operations.

Important inference from the state resources:

- I did not find a Michigan state professional license specifically for ordinary website design/development services,
- but that is not a guarantee that you have no local or activity-specific requirements.

## Phase 6: Insurance and Contracts

Before taking real clients:

- use a written service agreement,
- use a content authorization form,
- use an invoice with clear payment terms,
- get quotes for general liability and errors-and-omissions coverage.

## Phase 7: If You Hire Anyone

If you hire employees, Michigan employer rules start to matter quickly.

Examples:

- UIA registration,
- wage and hour posting requirements,
- workers' compensation rules.

Michigan says workers' compensation insurance is required for:

- all private employers regularly employing 3 or more employees at one time, or
- 1 or more employees 35 hours or more per week for 13 weeks or longer during the preceding 52 weeks.

If you stay solo, this section usually does not apply yet.

## Michigan Outreach Rules You Should Know

Michigan has adopted the federal Do Not Call Registry as the state's Do Not Call list for consumers.

Michigan's public telemarketing guidance includes:

- a general rule that telemarketers may not call consumers between `9:00 p.m.` and `8:00 a.m.`,
- a penal-code section saying unsolicited commercial telephone calls may not be made between `9:00 p.m.` and `9:00 a.m.`

For your business model, the safest calling rules are:

- make manual calls only,
- call public business lines, not scraped personal cell numbers,
- do not use robocalls,
- keep your own do-not-call list,
- stop if asked not to call again,
- stay inside normal business hours instead of trying to call near either cutoff.
