# Website Handoff and Domain Transfer Guide

This is the practical answer to your question:

Do you give the client the domain, the code, or both?

## Best Practice

The best default is:

- the client owns the domain,
- the client controls the final hosting or clearly approved managed hosting account,
- the client gets admin access,
- the client also receives a source copy of the site files or repository export,
- you keep support access only if they hire you for maintenance.

So the answer is usually:

Do not make "just the code" the handoff.

The proper handoff is usually:

1. domain control,
2. hosting/admin control,
3. website files or repository copy,
4. written launch and handoff confirmation.

## Why Giving Only the Code Is Usually Not Enough

A client cannot fully control the website if you only send them code but keep:

- the domain in your account,
- the hosting in your account,
- the DNS in your account,
- the form notifications in your account,
- analytics and search tools in your account.

That creates confusion and future disputes.

## Default Ownership Rule You Should Use

Unless a signed contract says otherwise:

- the client should own the final domain name,
- the client should have direct access to the registrar,
- the client should have direct access to the hosting,
- the client should have direct access to business email, analytics, and form destinations,
- your reusable framework, starter code, and internal process can remain yours.

## Your Best Operating Model

Use this model for almost every small business client:

### Domain

- bought in the client's name,
- registered with the client's email,
- billing method controlled by the client,
- you added as a manager or delegated user if the registrar allows it.

### Hosting

- opened in the client's name when practical,
- or clearly documented as managed hosting if you host multiple clients under your own service.

### Code / Files

- deployed live,
- backed up,
- exported as a ZIP or repository archive for the client,
- transferred after final payment if your contract says ownership transfers then.

## The Best Way to Handle Domains

## Best Option: Client Buys the Domain From the Start

This is the cleanest option.

Steps:

1. Tell the client which domain to buy.
2. Have the client create the registrar account with their own email.
3. Have the client add you as a delegate, manager, or temporary admin if the registrar supports it.
4. Point the DNS where the website needs to go.
5. Before launch, make sure the client can log in and receives renewal notices.

Why this is best:

- no transfer mess,
- no argument over ownership,
- no renewal surprise,
- easier handoff.

## Second-Best Option: You Buy It Temporarily, Then Transfer Control

Sometimes you will move faster by securing the domain yourself.

If you do that:

- say so clearly in the agreement,
- say whether the client is reimbursing the registration cost,
- say when the domain will be transferred,
- do not leave the domain in your name long term unless you are intentionally selling managed service with written terms.

## Two Ways Domain Handoffs Usually Happen

### Method A: Change Control Inside the Same Registrar

This is usually the easiest option.

Example:

- you registered the domain at Namecheap or GoDaddy,
- the client creates an account at that same registrar,
- you push the domain to the client's account or update the registrant/admin ownership using the registrar's internal process.

This is often simpler than moving registrars.

### Method B: Transfer to a Different Registrar

ICANN says transfers between registrars usually require:

- authorization by the domain holder,
- an AuthInfo or authorization code,
- cooperation from the losing and gaining registrars.

ICANN also says a domain generally cannot be transferred to a different registrar within:

- 60 days of initial registration,
- 60 days of a previous transfer.

ICANN also warns that changing registrant information can trigger a 60-day transfer lock with some registrars.

Practical meaning:

- if you think the client wants a different registrar, transfer first before changing registrant details when possible,
- or keep the domain at the current registrar and just move account control.

## My Recommendation on Domains

For your business, the safest default rule is:

1. client buys the domain from the start whenever possible,
2. if you bought it first, push it to the client at the same registrar rather than forcing an inter-registrar transfer,
3. document the transfer in writing.

## The Best Way to Hand Off the Website Itself

After the build is complete, your handoff should cover more than one item.

### 1. Live Site Access

Give the client:

- website admin URL if applicable,
- hosting login or delegated access,
- DNS access if they control the registrar,
- form inbox destination confirmation.

### 2. Source Files or Repository

Give the client one of these:

- ZIP export of the production-ready site files,
- Git repository transfer,
- Git repository archive,
- CMS export if the site is built on a platform that supports exports.

### 3. Credentials and Renewals

Confirm in writing:

- who renews the domain,
- who renews hosting,
- who receives security notices,
- who handles backups,
- who handles content edits after launch.

### 4. Documentation

Deliver a short handoff note with:

- domain registrar,
- hosting provider,
- DNS location,
- site platform,
- analytics location,
- form destination email,
- maintenance status,
- support contact.

## Your Default Handoff Workflow

Use this every time:

1. Receive final approval in writing.
2. Receive final payment.
3. Confirm domain ownership and registrar access.
4. Confirm hosting access and billing ownership.
5. Launch the site.
6. Test forms, phone links, SSL, and analytics.
7. Export a backup copy of the website.
8. Transfer or confirm access to registrar, hosting, and site admin.
9. Send a handoff email listing all systems and who owns them.
10. Move into maintenance or close the project.

## Contract Rules You Should Use

Your agreement should say:

- when ownership transfers,
- whether you retain rights to reusable templates and starter code,
- whether domain registration is client-owned or temporarily provider-managed,
- whether hosting is client-owned or managed by you,
- what happens if the client stops paying for maintenance,
- what handoff items are included.

## My Recommendation for You Specifically

Because you are starting out, your cleanest business model is:

- client owns domain,
- client owns registrar account,
- client owns hosting or clearly approved managed hosting,
- you deliver the live website plus a backup/export,
- you keep maintenance access only under a monthly plan.

That is usually better than keeping everything in your account and "giving code later."
